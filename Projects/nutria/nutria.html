Case Study

Turning invisible
churn into
earlier intervention

A retention-focused dashboard concept for independent nutritionists, reframed from analytics screen to decision engine.

Role

Product Designer

Timeline

3 weeks

Goal

Help nutritionists identify churn risk earlier and take action faster

The Dashboard is coming soon

Context_

Patient dropout is a well-documented challenge in nutrition and weight management programs, with studies reporting attrition rates ranging from 10% to over 80%, often limiting treatment effectiveness. Existing tools track care delivery well but ignore retention entirely. The data was there but the decisions weren't.

I shifted the dashboard from displaying more data to highlighting fewer, more meaningful decisions. The key hypothesis was that retention is a behavioral challenge, not a data problem.

The outcome is a three-layer decision flow: Awareness → Prioritization → Action, built around how practitioners rapidly scan information and make decisions under time pressure, often with limited attention between sessions, as observed in user research.

The Real Problem_

Nutritionists already had data. What they didn't have were decisions.

What tools do well

  • Store data
  • Show trends
  • Track meal plan adherence

What tools don't do

  • Tell the practitioner which patient is slipping
  • Explain why
  • Suggest what to do next
Result

Nutritionists react too late, when the patient is already gone. This wasn't a visibility problem. It was a framing problem. Data was available but wasn't organized around a decision.

Discovery & Insight_

Three insights that shaped direction

Across both global and Brazilian platforms, the pattern is consistent: tools are optimized for managing care, not for deciding how to prevent patient drop-off.

What's well-served vs. what's ignored across nutrition platforms.

01.

Metrics without meaning don't drive action.

Dashboards already show numbers. What was missing was the context that turns a number into a decision.

02.

Nutritionists scan. They don't read.

Practitioners review patient status in short intervals between sessions, relying on quick scanning rather than deep analysis.

03.

Retention shows up as patterns, not scores.

Early disengagement doesn’t appear as a single risk score, it emerges through behavioral patterns over time.

Product Thinking Shift_

From

"What data should we show?"

↓
To

"What decision should this enable?"

This shift reframed the entire product approach, from how information was structured to what features were necessary and how decisions were prioritized.

Strategy & Product Decisions_

Four decisions that defined the product

01

Reframe the dashboard as a decision engine, not a report.

Most platforms show everything. I chose the opposite, show fewer things, but rank them by what needs a decision today. This meant cutting generic practice analytics entirely from scope.

02

Structure the IA around three decision layers.

  • Awareness: what's happening now (pulse check in <5s)
  • Prioritization: who needs action first (triage list)
  • Insight / Action: why, and how to respond (patient-level context)

Target: 80% of retention decisions happen in the first two layers. Only escalate to deep context when the practitioner has a specific reason.

03

Surface behavioral patterns, not static scores.

The IA shows the signal first, with the score as supporting context, not the other way around. Evidence builds trust. Judgment erodes it.

Instead of

"70% risk score"

→
Show this

"7 days inactive + missed check-in + adherence dropping"

04

Keep AI in the loop, but not in charge.

AI-assisted explanations and suggested actions reduce cognitive load. But the intervention decision stays with the nutritionist. In healthcare, trust breaks fast when automation overreaches. Better to recommend than to auto-act.

Trade-offs & Prioritization_

What I chose. What I cut. Why.

Every product bet is a prioritization decision. Depth over breadth was the consistent logic across all five choices.

Chose Cut Reasoning
Retention-only scope Generic practice analytics Narrow problem = sharper product. Retention was the underserved gap across the entire market.
Behavioral pattern signals Static risk scores as primary UI Patterns feel like evidence; scores feel like judgment. Trust follows evidence.
AI-suggested actions Full automation Healthcare needs practitioner-in-loop. Overconfident automation damages trust faster than it saves time.
Three-layer IA Feature-rich side nav Layered flow matches how practitioners actually decide under time pressure.
One core task (retention) Adjacent jobs (billing, meal planning, scheduling) A 3-week concept wins by going deep on one decision, not wide on many.

Solution_

The decisions made visible

01 Awareness layer

Home Dashboard

Job: "What needs my attention today?"

A high-signal pulse check, total patients, % at risk, adherence trend, today's focus. Stacks by urgency, not chronology. Designed to be understood in under 5 seconds, not studied.

02 Prioritization layer

Smart Alerts

Job: "Which patients are slipping, and what's the signal?"

Triage list grouped by risk level. Each row surfaces the key behavioral signal (e.g., "7d inactivity"), a suggested next action, and a last-seen timestamp. Reduces 100+ patients to the handful that matter this week.

03 Insight / Action layer

Patient Profile

Job: "What changed, how serious is it, what should I do?"

A behavioral timeline, not a score card. Shows which behaviors are driving risk (check-in drop, reduced messaging, adherence plateau, app silence) so the intervention can be matched to the cause.

04 Action

Intervention Modal

Job: "Respond before drop-off."

Context-aware suggestion (message · schedule · adjust plan), matched to the signal. One-tap action. Decision stays with the practitioner; the product reduces the cost of acting on what they already know.

How success would be measured_

Three KPI layers, mirroring the decision flow

Layer 1

Visibility

Are users noticing risk earlier?

  • Time to identify a flagged patient
  • Dashboard-to-alert click-through rate
  • Flagged patients reviewed per week
Why it matters

Awareness is the first goal. If users aren't seeing risk signals early enough, the rest of the flow doesn't matter.

Layer 2

Actionability

Are users acting once risk is surfaced?

  • Alert-to-profile click-through rate
  • % flagged patients receiving follow-up
  • Time from alert to intervention
  • Adoption of suggested actions
Why it matters

The core value is helping users respond before churn happens. If users see alerts but don't act, the workflow is failing.

Layer 3

Retention Impact

Does earlier visibility lead to better retention?

  • Retention of flagged patients post-intervention
  • Recovery rate
  • Plan renewal rate
  • Revenue recovered from at-risk patients
Why it matters

This is business impact. Long-term success depends on whether earlier intervention actually reduces avoidable churn.

Reflection_

What started as a data problem revealed itself as a decision problem. The real shift was moving from designing for visibility to designing for action, structuring the product around what needs to be decided, not what can be displayed. That change redefined how value is created in the product.

The skill I'd carry forward to any SaaS or healthcare product is to turn invisible signals into clear decisions without adding noise.
